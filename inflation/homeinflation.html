INFLATION​ :


                  
                An Overview Inflation refers to the sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. In India, inflation is a critical economic indicator, and policymakers closely monitor it to ensure price stability and sustainable economic growth. The primary agency responsible for monetary policy, including managing inflation, is the Reserve Bank of India (RBI).

Forms of INFLATION

Demand Pull Inflation

Monetary Inflation

Hyper  Inflation

Cost-Push Inflation

Structural Inflation

  • ☑
    This type of inflation occurs when aggregate demand for goods and services exceeds aggregate supply. Factors such as increased consumer spending, government expenditure, or private investment can lead to demand-pull inflation. Rapid economic growth and high levels of consumer confidence are often associated with this type of inflation. 
  • ☑
    Cost-push inflation is driven by an increase in production costs, such as higher wages, raw material prices, or energy costs. External factors, such as global commodity price fluctuations, can also contribute to cost-push inflation. In India, factors like fuel price volatility and changes in agricultural input costs can contribute to this type of inflation. 
  • ☑
    Structural inflation is rooted in long-term structural issues within the economy. In India, structural factors such as inefficiencies in supply chains, inadequate infrastructure, and regulatory constraints can contribute to persistent inflationary pressures. 
  • ☑
    Monetary inflation occurs when there is an increase in the money supply in the economy. Central banks, including the Reserve Bank of India, play a crucial role in controlling monetary inflation through monetary policy tools such as interest rates and open market operations. 
  • ☑
    While hyperinflation is an extreme form of inflation characterized by extremely rapid and uncontrollable price increases, it has not been a prevalent issue in India. Hyperinflation is often associated with severe economic instability, which India has generally avoided.
